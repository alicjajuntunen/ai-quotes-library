
## Taste is the human edge

> "Taste is a shorthand for 'this person has it and they miraculously somehow were born with it.' It's very very much not true... taste equals care. All of that stuff is care. It's taking the time to anticipate, to simulate, to consider how other people might feel, to put yourself in other people's shoes. And yes, like we say it's taste at the end, but it's just hard work."
> — [[Loredana Crisan - Figma's big bets for the future of AI design]]

> "There is no shortcut to having taste or developing a high-craft, high-velocity practice. With AI, we have shortcuts to creating a lot of things, but at the end of the day it comes back to discovering and learning the language of visual design."
> — [[Andy Madrick - Why AI changed design handoff forever]]

> "Our job now, with limitless UI out there, is how do we take it and make a restrained version where form follows function and we're avoiding decoration for decoration's sake."
> — [[Andy Madrick - Why AI changed design handoff forever]]

> "Ultimately the goal is that I want the app to feel like it was designed by one hand and not 40. That is incredibly hard to do. It's kind of impossible, but that's the goal."
> — [[Polly D'Arcy - Going from IC to VP of Design at Wealthsimple]]

> "These small UI bugs really do matter because over time they're paper cuts and they wear down that trust battery."
> — [[Polly D'Arcy - Going from IC to VP of Design at Wealthsimple]]

> "I talk about me dictating a painting over the phone. It's very, very hard... If there's a color that I want to put in my design, the color wheel will never be replaced by a prompt."
> — [[Loredana Crisan - Figma's big bets for the future of AI design]]

> "This is what AI is actually really, really good at: Robot Monkey work. Freeing designers to do what actual humans do best. Human work. Empathy. Story. Communication. Invention. Innovation."
> — [[UX for AI - Shorthand UX Design Notation as AI Prompt]]

> "humor is the last frontier that no AI can really touch and humor comes from weird places in your mind of some weird mix of trauma pattern matching and absurdism"
> — [[How a non-technical founder built a $100K ARR meme company - Jason Levin (Memelord CEO)]]

> "It gets to 80%. But like that taste, that craft is like that's why designers will always exist in my opinion. Like they know how to elevate the experience... it's not going to be perfect. And like we are here to steer them."

> — [[Brett Williams - How a Visual Designer Became a Builder]]

> "These LLMs are not very good at the last mile of design. And that's still our superpower, needs to be our superpower. Our engineers will bring it all the way up to 80 to 90%, and then it's up to us as designers to just own the outcome."
> — [[Andy Madrick - Why AI changed design handoff forever]]

> "We can probably get to a seven out of 10 out of the box... but that just means you have more room to care and put love and intention and thought into the things that you build."

> "I think we have no excuse anymore to not be responsible for those changes that we want to see... these digital artifacts are extensions of our work and our practice. And we have the ability now to just own how that gets delivered."

## When everyone can build, judgment wins

> "What's stopping a competitor from literally vibe coding the same exact thing tomorrow? It'll take them a day or two. So finding the customers, and having a really differentiating product that feels more premium and has an emotional connection with its users, those are things that fall squarely in the design world."
> — [[Ron Goldin - Building your ideas as a design leader]]

> "You have a lot of non-designers right now looking at the outputs of AI tools and they're like, oh, that's pretty good. But a lot of them look very similar. And that is because they're literally all being trained on the same world of stuff."
> — [[Ron Goldin - Building your ideas as a design leader]]

> "You can't just be execution. You can't just be order takers... that is no longer valuable because a product manager doesn't need you anymore to do that."
> — [[Ron Goldin - Building your ideas as a design leader]]

> "These tools make competence cheap for everyone... So a good designer is going to figure out how to make systems to harness that design work and use it rather than be like, no, that sucks."

> "Just because everyone can ship doesn't mean not everything should ship... as everyone has the ability to access code and push to production, we need better systems that scale."

> "When everybody can build anything, it becomes super clear who has great judgment and who doesn't."
> — [[Polly D'Arcy - Going from IC to VP of Design at Wealthsimple]]

> — [[Loredana Crisan - Figma's big bets for the future of AI design]]

## Quiet AI beats the magic text box

> "Perhaps the best AI in 2025 is "quiet" — without any sparkles or chatbots. It just sits behind a humble button or runs in the background, doing the tedious job that users had to slowly do in the past. It shines when it fixes actual problems that it has, not when it screams for attention that it doesn't deserve."
> — [[Designing AI Interfaces (Notion)]]

> "The issue isn't the lack of design skills or implementation hurdles: it's the product-market-fit gap that is often overlooked or dismissed in favor of a super-intelligent text box."
> — [[Designing AI Interfaces (Notion)]]

> "Probably one of the most underrated yet impactful design patterns for AI interfaces is the ability to tune AI experience. This could show itself as a style lenses or temperature knobs — little tools to help users request a personalized output easier."
